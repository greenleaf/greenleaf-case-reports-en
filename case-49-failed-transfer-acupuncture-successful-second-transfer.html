After One Failed Transfer, the One Change That Led to a Healthy Baby

Successful transfer after 8 weeks of care Fertility Uterine Conditions

Her first embryo transfer failed, and no one could tell her why. When she asked her fertility specialist what had gone wrong, the answer was simply that it might have been "a bad cycle" — a reason, but not really an explanation. On top of the disappointment, she'd also just learned during her fertility workup that she had endometriosis, something she hadn't known about until that point.

A closer look at her overall pattern told a fuller story. She had trouble sleeping, tension along both sides of her ribs, tenderness across the center of her chest, headaches strong enough to be disruptive, and loose stools that tended to show up specifically around her period. Taken together, this pointed to a nervous system running in a persistent state of heightened stress — the kind that can affect hormonal rhythm, digestion, and sleep all at once, and one that may also have been contributing to the inflammatory environment that comes with endometriosis.

Same Body, Same Diagnosis — What Was Different the Second Time

When she came in for her first visit, she was understandably anxious about what the endometriosis diagnosis might mean for her chances. She was reassured that this was manageable, and treatment began that same day. What's notable about this case is what she changed between her first transfer and her second: nothing in her diet or daily habits. Acupuncture was the only addition.

Calming an Overworked Nervous System Ahead of Transfer

Treatment focused on settling the nervous system tension that was showing up across so many systems at once — the rib-side and chest tightness, the headaches, the disrupted sleep — while also supporting healthy blood flow to the uterus in the weeks leading up to and around her transfer. Improving circulation to the uterine lining and reducing the inflammatory load tied to endometriosis are two of the more consistent ways acupuncture is thought to support IVF outcomes, particularly when a first cycle hasn't gone as expected.

A Different Outcome, With Everything Else the Same

Her second transfer was successful. She is now the mother of a baby who recently turned three months old — and by her own account, acupuncture was the one variable that had changed between the two cycles.

What This Case Left Behind

Fertility clinics can tell a patient a transfer failed, but they can't always tell her why — and "it might just have been a bad cycle" is a real answer, but rarely a satisfying one. This case is a reminder that the body's broader stress signals — poor sleep, chest and rib-side tension, headaches, digestive changes tied to the menstrual cycle — aren't separate from fertility outcomes, even when a fertility clinic isn't set up to look at them together. Addressing that whole pattern, alongside the medical treatment already underway, can meaningfully change what happens next.
