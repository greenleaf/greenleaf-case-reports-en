Twenty Sleepless Nights, and the Depression That Followed

Recovered in 9 weeks Mental Health

He was in his forties, and exhaustion showed on his face the moment he sat down.

He'd been working in the US when the stress became unbearable. Twenty nights without sleep, and it finally landed him in the ER, where a shot was the only thing that got him to sleep. Depression settled in right after that, deep enough that any motivation for work simply vanished. He came to Canada hoping some time off would help.

But rest alone wasn't enough. Every morning, the moment he woke up, and every night, right before falling asleep — those were the two low points that repeated daily. His pulse used to spike past 110, and had settled around 100 lately. At his first visit, his heart rate was 101, blood pressure 116/79.

He had a large build, ran hot enough that he'd kick off the blankets at night, and sweated heavily. His heart raced often. Digestion was fine, but his stools stayed loose, and he woke two or three times a night to urinate.

The one thing the brain hates most

Leave a computer running too long and the CPU heats up. A fan kicks in. If that's still not enough, the whole system slows itself down to cope. The brain works in a strikingly similar way — of all the organs in the body, it's the most sensitive to heat. Whatever the cause, once internal heat starts to rise, the brain is the first to notice, and the first to sound the alarm.

Depression is one form that alarm takes. The loss of motivation, the heaviness, the pull toward doing nothing at all — this isn't a failure of willpower. It reads more like the brain saying, "cool this down first, before anything else gets your energy."

In his case, the heat, the heavy sweating, the pulse that wouldn't come down — they were all pointing in the same direction. His internal heat had climbed past where it should sit, and the brain kept sending that signal out in the form of depression.

The organ that manufactures heat

So where was this heat coming from? In his case, the liver.

Most people think of the liver purely as a detox organ, but it's also the body's biggest heat-generating factory. Under extreme stress, or when the body's balance tips, this factory starts producing more heat than it should. That excess heat travels up to the brain and keeps the alarm ringing.

So the direction of treatment was clear: bring down the excess heat being generated by the liver. Gardenia fruit was the key herb for that job, paired with an acupuncture point that specifically targets liver heat.

Body heat isn't the same as a fever

One distinction matters here. This kind of "elevated body heat" isn't the same as a fever from a cold or the flu. A thermometer might read perfectly normal. It's closer to heat building up inside — particularly from organs like the liver — and continuously stirring up the nervous system and circulation.

This distinction matters even more because it tends to fall through the cracks of a Western medical framework. Body temperature checks out normal, there's no sign of infection, so the idea of "having heat" sounds unfamiliar, even confusing. But the elevated pulse, the heavy sweating, running hot, and the recurring depression were all symptoms tied back to this same internal heat.

Pulse 75, and the depression lifted

As treatment brought down the excess heat coming from the liver, he reported for the first time that his depression had noticeably eased.

By the next visit, the improvement was clear enough that he could feel it himself. And his pulse finally settled at 75 — squarely in the normal range.

A week later, his pulse was still holding at 75, and he said the depression had all but disappeared. Treatment wrapped up that day.

Once the internal heat came down, the brain's alarm went quiet along with it.
