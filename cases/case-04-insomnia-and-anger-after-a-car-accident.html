Insomnia and Anger That Started After a Car Accident

Recovered in 6 weeks Mental Health

Jane's face was already flushed by the time she sat down. Acne stood out along her cheeks, and there was an edge in her voice she clearly couldn't smooth over.

"When I'm around my husband, my chest just seizes up. And I can't sleep."

It had started six months earlier, with marital tension as the trigger. But what really pushed things over the edge was something else entirely: a car accident that May. Everything got worse after that. Then, not long after, she witnessed another crash on the road.

"In that moment, my own accident played out right in front of me again, exactly as it happened."

From there, sleep became nearly impossible. Her heart raced. She sighed constantly without meaning to. Her appetite disappeared and nausea crept in. Heat surged through her, and cold sweat broke out on her scalp and back. Her face stayed flushed, and everything about her — the tone, the posture — read like someone holding back anger with both hands.

A fork in the road

Racing heart, insomnia, and digestive trouble as a set usually points to heat that's built up and gotten stuck around the chest. But in Jane's case, there was something else layered on top: the vivid, intrusive replay of the accident — a trauma response. The real question was which of the two was closer to the root. I started by targeting the heat trapped in the chest.

A week later, the response was clear. Sleep came easier, the racing heart and tightness eased, and her appetite came back. Then, at the next visit, everything flipped. Digestion was still fine, but sleep had suddenly become impossible again, and the heat and cold sweats had gotten worse.

This time I shifted the formula to address the trauma response instead. The effect was fast — within three days, sleep came easily again, and the sweating eased. But now the racing heart was back.

Three swings before landing

Over the following weeks, we went back and forth between the two formulas. Fix the sleep, and the racing heart lingered. Fix the racing heart, and something else popped up. One pattern kept repeating: any time she drove for more than thirty minutes, irritation and anger would flare up without fail — a reflex the accident had left behind, triggered by nothing more than her hands on the wheel.

Eventually we returned to the formula that had worked first. Two weeks later, on a check-in call, her answer was short.

"The anger's gone. The racing heart's gone. And I'm still sleeping fine."

After weeks of swinging between two paths, the answer turned out to be right where she'd started.
