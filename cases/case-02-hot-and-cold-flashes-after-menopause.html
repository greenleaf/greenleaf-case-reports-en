The Anxiety That Started Right After Menopause

Recovered in 6 weeks Uterine Conditions

Carol carried a notebook everywhere. She wrote down everything I said, word for word, and asked precise, well-formed questions when something didn't add up. But when it came to her own body, she had nothing figured out.

"This isn't like me at all."

Before menopause, she told me, she'd been the fit, energetic, up-for-anything type. Then, on a recent island getaway with friends, everyone else was buzzing about what to do that day — and she just couldn't bring herself to care. It rattled her. "I have no idea why I'm like this," she said.

Her body was sending mixed signals too. She'd feel a wave of heat, then suddenly turn cold. She sweated through the day and, worse, through the night — enough to wake her four or five times. Her mouth was constantly dry; she was gulping water all day. Constipation had gotten bad enough that she'd started taking magnesium for it. She'd already spent a year on herbs from another practitioner with barely any change.

Two kinds of heat, one answer

When the body's temperature control loses its footing after menopause, hot flashes and cold spells can show up back to back, sometimes within the same hour. That was exactly Carol's pattern. The tricky part was that these two opposite sensations don't always come from the same place. Given how much stamina she still had, how thirsty she was, and the constipation, the first read pointed toward heat pooling somewhere it shouldn't — so that's where the first formula aimed.

A week later: half a win. The cold spells vanished completely, and the thirst eased. But the heat waves hadn't budged, and something new had surfaced — a low, heavy sadness she hadn't mentioned before.

From here it became a process of elimination. If cooling the heat had only solved half the problem, the other half had to be coming from somewhere else. Her digestion was fine, but her hands ran cold — so the formula shifted again. Still not much movement. We adjusted once more, this time zeroing in on the night sweats, the tightness in her chest, and the broken sleep.

That third adjustment is when things finally turned. The hot flashes dropped to about once a day. She stopped waking at night. Ten days later, she said something I still think about.

"The anxiety, the sadness that made no sense — it's gone. I actually want to feel excited about something again."

Chasing down the heat had, in the end, brought back the warmth.
