A Volleyball Shoulder Injury That Wouldn't Lift on Its Own, Resolved in Three Weeks

Improvement over 3 weeks Pain

Mid-swing on a volleyball spike, a sharp pain shot through the outside of the shoulder. From that moment on, trying to raise the arm brought a dull ache that started at the outer shoulder and ran all the way down to the elbow. At night, the pain grew worse, making it nearly impossible to sleep on that side. This was exactly the pattern this patient was living with.

The Difference Between Lifting It Yourself and Having It Lifted

One detail stood out clearly in this case. When the patient tried to raise the arm using her own strength, it barely moved and hurt in the process. But when someone else took hold of the arm and lifted it for her, it moved far more easily. That contrast isn't a coincidence. The supraspinatus plays a central role in actively raising the arm — when it's injured, the muscle can't generate the force needed for active movement. But passive movement doesn't depend on the muscle generating force at all; since the joint itself is intact, it can still move relatively freely when moved from the outside.

An Injury Born From a Powerful Motion

A forceful, overhead motion like a volleyball spike or serve places a sudden, heavy load on the supraspinatus. When that load exceeds what the tendon can handle, some of its fibers can tear or become damaged. The injured area produces that dull ache running from the outer shoulder down the arm, and lying on that side at night compresses the area further, intensifying the pain and disrupting sleep.

Even With a Tear, Recovery Can Move Quickly

A supraspinatus injury doesn't automatically mean a long road to recovery — but how long recovery takes depends heavily on the extent of the damage. When a tear is extensive enough to require surgery, recovery can take anywhere from three to six months. In this case, the injury was fortunately localized rather than extensive, which meant surgery wasn't necessary — and that was a key factor behind the fast recovery. Acupuncture and cupping were used together, focused on improving circulation around the injured tissue and supporting the healing process. Acupuncture directly addressed tension at and around the site of injury, while cupping drew blood flow up to the surface tissue, supporting the circulation the tissue needed to recover.

Three Weeks, and the Arm Lifted on Its Own Again

Over three weeks of treatment, an arm that had initially struggled to lift on its own gradually recovered, and the pain that had disrupted sleep every night eased along with it. Rather than being discouraged by a diagnosis of supraspinatus injury, staying consistent with the right treatment led to a fast recovery in this case.
