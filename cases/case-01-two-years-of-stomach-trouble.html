Digestive Issues

Two Years of Stomach Trouble No Test Could Explain

Recovered in 3 weeks Digestive Issues

Michelle walked in already talking. Before she'd even sat down, she'd told me she'd been fighting her stomach for two years.

"My doctor did an endoscopy. Nothing showed up. I've tried probiotics, digestive enzymes, everything on the shelf. None of it sticks."

She was a woman in her fifties, originally from the Philippines. Small frame, and a worry that seemed to live permanently at the edges of her face. The more we talked, the clearer it became that this wasn't ordinary indigestion. Stress made it worse. So did anything oily, greasy, or sour. A recent trip home to visit family had wrecked her stomach for the entire week. At night, she woke like clockwork between 1 and 2:30 a.m. — and if anything was on her mind, getting back to sleep wasn't happening.

Then she said something that stuck with me.

"Right here" — she pointed just below her breastbone — "it's like there's a butterfly flapping around. I don't know how else to describe it."

Not quite pain. Not quite nothing. Just enough to keep bothering her.

Reading the whole pattern, not just the complaint

This is where the thinking in the room shifts. A stomach that won't settle doesn't automatically mean reaching for something to settle the stomach. The real question is what pattern this particular stomach trouble is sitting inside of.

With Michelle, several threads pointed the same direction. She was running to the bathroom every hour. She worried constantly. Her lips were dry, and she'd get a bitter taste in her mouth now and then. There was tenderness along her side when I pressed. And, of course, the butterfly.

Put simply: her body was stuck with the alarm on. The racing thoughts, the broken sleep, the frequent urination, the fluttering under the ribs — these weren't five separate problems. They were one system running hot. When the body's threat response won't switch off, muscles stay braced, the bladder gets twitchy, and digestion — never a priority in emergency mode — gets shoved to the back of the line. Of course her stomach wasn't working properly. It wasn't supposed to be, not with the alarm going off around the clock.

The prescription, three weeks later

I gave her a formula built to turn that alarm off. Three weeks, three times a day.

She came back a different person.

"The indigestion's gone. No more heartburn. I'm not running to the bathroom every hour anymore. And I'm sleeping — I don't wake up at one anymore."

The tenderness by her ribs had disappeared too. What struck me was what came next: even though every symptom had cleared, she was flying home to the Philippines the following week and asked for two more weeks of herbs — just in case, she said, remembering how badly the last trip had gone. She came back once more after that trip, this time for a cold. Her stomach, she confirmed, hadn't given her any trouble at all.

She's stopped in a few times since, for a stiff back, for a cold. Every time, I find myself asking anyway:

"How's the stomach these days?"

"Oh, that? I completely forgot about it."

The butterfly had gone quiet.
