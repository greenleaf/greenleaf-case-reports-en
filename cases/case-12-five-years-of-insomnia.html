Five Years of Insomnia, and a Mind That Wouldn't Switch Off at Night

Recovered in 10 weeks Mental Health Cancer & Chemotherapy

She was in her eighties, and had been living with the same problem for five years. Her body was clearly exhausted, but the moment she lay down, her mind snapped wide awake.

She'd been through treatment for breast cancer, and her insomnia had gotten noticeably worse ever since. Despite drinking plenty of water, her mouth stayed dry, and she was up often to urinate. Her stools ran loose, and every so often, she felt a strange hollowness in the center of her chest. Her eyes felt dry, her shoulders and upper back ached often, and her legs sometimes went a little numb.

At first glance, she looked like any grandmother you'd expect to meet — but there was a faint flush to her face, and her temperament ran quick and sensitive. She tensed up easily.

Two systems, both out of sync

Two separate problems were layered on top of each other in her body.

One was the switch that lets the body stand down for the night. Normally, once evening comes, the body gets the signal that it's safe to rest, and settles into a calmer state. But in her case, an undercurrent of tension and sensitivity ran so constantly in the background that this switch struggled to flip off, even at night. Fatigue and falling asleep, it turned out, run on two different systems — and her case made that distinction obvious.

The other problem was how fluid was distributed through her body. Despite drinking as much water as she did, her mouth stayed dry, and she needed the bathroom often. This wasn't a shortage of fluid — it was a distribution problem, with fluid pooling in some places and running short in others.

Together, these two things kept her wide awake, night after night.

Resetting the direction

The faint flush on her face suggested heat sitting somewhere inside, so treatment started there, aiming to cool that down. But the very first night on the herbs, she felt even more tense lying down, and sleep came even harder than before. Her body was sending a clear signal: this wasn't the right direction.

Taking that cue, treatment shifted toward calming the nighttime "stand-down" switch while also correcting the fluid distribution at the same time. The first combination in that direction didn't move the needle much. But once the fluid-balancing component was given more weight relative to the rest, things changed.

Nights that came easier

Once both systems were being addressed together, she reported falling asleep far more easily. The dry mouth and frequent urination hadn't changed much, but the insomnia — the thing that had bothered her most — was clearly improving.

Her sleep kept getting better from there. I suggested she continue a bit longer, but she said, "I think this is enough," and declined. She finished out what she'd already been prescribed and wrapped up treatment there.
